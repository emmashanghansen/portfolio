Case study

Norwegian Game Awards

Redesigning the website for Norway's biggest student game development award show — so that visitors could actually tell what it was.

Organisation

Designhjelpen

My role

Project lead, design, Webflow

Team

4 people

The problem

Winning at NGA is something to be proud of. Linking to the official website was not.

The Norwegian Game Awards is Norway's biggest award show for student game developers. But first-time visitors questioned whether they'd landed on the right page — or whether NGA was even a real competition. Basic deadlines and submission requirements were buried across multiple pages of dense paragraphs. And for potential sponsors seeing the site for the first time, there was nothing to communicate what kind of event this actually was.

The problems were two distinct but related: confusing navigation and buried information for participants, and a site that failed to establish credibility for anyone arriving without prior context.

How might we communicate to visitors what type of event NGA is?

Process

This was an open problem with few hard constraints, which meant the whole team could contribute meaningfully from the start. Rather than moving straight to solutions, we each stated our unbiased personal read on the problem first. The goal was to surface where our assumptions aligned and where they diverged, then reach a shared understanding before defining the brief.

For learning the tool, the approach was direct: make something. We produced a new Figma sketch every meeting, which turned iteration into practice.

Shaping the brief

What was clear

Some improvements were straightforward. The audience expected a dark mode, and the event called for a playful visual register. Both were easy to align on.

Video vs animation

NGA wanted videos on the site to provide movement and convey information. I pushed back. Videos frequently broke, loaded slowly, and most visitors aren't going to sit through one when they're looking for specific information. I proposed site animations instead — lighter, more reliable, and less disruptive to the overall browsing experience. NGA agreed.

The contact form

The existing contact form felt dated and took up space. I assumed few people used it and wanted to remove it. NGA said people did use it, so it stayed. After delivery, they removed it themselves — replacing it with an email call-to-action. Clicking a button and writing a message is less effort than filling out a form. In hindsight, I was right about the direction, but I should have validated the assumption with data before pushing the point.

The game-levels roadmap

At one point I considered a roadmap that looked like game levels — on theme, memorable, and genuinely fun. I decided against it. NGA needed to be able to maintain the site independently. Building something that required specialist knowledge to update every year would mean it'd be outdated within months of handoff. I'd rather build something that outlasts the project than something that impresses during delivery.

Wireframes or brief notes showing these decisions being worked through

Solution

The visual identity moved from uninspiring grey and muddy green to punchy green on dark blue. Critical information — deadlines, how to participate — is visible in the hero section, with a clear call to action to register. Navigation is clearer, information is easier to find, and the hierarchy on each page does the work that long paragraphs used to bury.

Outside of formal project scope, I also gave the previous winners page a cleanup it badly needed.

Outcome

The site now reads as credible and inviting. The visual identity is consistent and immediately legible as belonging to a game development event. NGA were happy with the result.

Everyone we spoke with after delivery — potential participants and people seeing it for the first time — said it looked and felt significantly better. The feedback was qualitative, but the core problem was that people didn't trust what they were looking at. That problem is visibly solved.

What I'd do differently

Not let the client overrule me so quickly on decisions I believed were right for the user. It's fine to challenge the client and advocate for the user — that's part of the job.

On any higher-stakes project: validate more assumptions with real users before implementation. Heuristics are useful, but they're still educated guesses. We didn't face consequences here, but it's better to verify.

Include post-delivery cleanup in the project scope from the start. After handoff, other projects took over and bugs I'd noticed never got fixed.
